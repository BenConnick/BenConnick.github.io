Breaking Expectations

24th August 2020 at 1:38am
V5

Overview

I want to set up situations in which the player has expectations of how it's going to go down, then I want to blow their mind by breaking the fundamental assumptions that they got here with.

Word of caution

That's a delicate balance, because you could just end up with a game that no one wants to interact with because it doesn't have any grounding. In order to break expectations, you have to set some up first.

Unreliable Narrator

Here's one method of breaking expectations: I want to challenge/tease the player with the idea that they can overcome the unreliable narrator by reading between the lines, only to reveal that it is actually the player who has been narrating.

Game can Turn Itself Off

Here's another method of breaking expectations: meta-narrative. There are certain assumptions that come with playing a game. Many of these have been explored already in games like Undertale and DDLC. Example: The player has an in-game dialogue option to wake up the Red King (having been warned that "we're all a part of the Red King's Dream; when he wakes up we'll all be gone"). Upon choosing this option, the game application just shuts down. (Showing the player to be the Red King all along)

There Can Be Color in the Black And White Game

This one is kinda self-explanatory, but if done well I think it could be really cool.

There Can Be 3D in a 2D Game

No examples yet

The Screen Can Be A Thing, Not Just A Window

For instance, you could do the gameplay as a RT and map that texture onto some UVs of a 3D such that it appears flat until the model moves. Or the screen could shatter like glass.

The Game Can Remember Things It Shouldn't

Like Undertale

Gameplay Can Be Dialogue

Example: while the player is falling down, the platforms could have dialogue lines or words on them.

Characters Can Remember Things That Haven't Happened Yet

Characters Can Show Up When They Are Not Allowed To

Characters Can Move While The Game Is Paused

The Music Can Be Diagetic

Characters could comment on the background music, or even modify it. It could also be revealed to be played by a character, or playing over speakers.

Sound Effects Could Be Something Different Than You Think

The player's footstep sound could continue after they stop walking, showing that what they thought was their footsteps all along was something following them.

Characters Can Address The Player Instead of The MC

This is 4th wall breaking 101 but I'm going to do this either constantly or at least once.

15th August 2020 at 12:06am
V1 V4

Early on, the world's governments tried to halt the rapid spread by shutting down all communication networks.

This was probably their best option, but in the sudden silence, panic ensued. Society as we know it collapsed, as our lives were abruptly sent back to the dark ages.

It was utter chaos. The governments were too scared of spreading Wonderland to explain what was going on.

Of course, with communications down, information about Wonderland was highly sought after, but this information came at a heavy price.

It is our greatest advantage as a species that we are curious. This is what made Wonderland such a dangerous opponent. To get close and to study it safely was nearly impossible, since exposing researchers to the infected only served to spread the infection.

Even so, we spearheaded an emergency research effort, in an attempt to understand the danger we faced. This was Project Rabbit Hole. Ultimately, we succeeded in compiling a confidential collection of all writings and images known to cause infection; the so-called "Wonderland Files".

Gameplay Spec

23rd August 2020 at 10:22pm
Gameplay V4 V5

Overview

RPG Mode

Reference Game: Deltarune

Perspective: Fake 3/4 orthographic so you can see a little side but mostly top of things.

Controls: WASD or direction keys / buttons to move avatar in 8 directions (diagonals allowed), space bar or return to interact / confirm

Challenge: Exploration. Environments contain hidden bits of lore that can be inspected or interacted with by pressing the "interact" button.

Platformer Mode

Reference Game: NS Shaft

Perspective: Side view (typical platformer perspective)

Controls: AD or direction keys / buttons to move avatar in horizontal directions. Unlike typical platformers there is no jump key. You can gain a little altitude using bounce pads.

Challenge: Dexterity. Avoid falling below the bottom of the screen, taking damage from spikes, or hitting the top of the screen.

Avatar Properties

  • no inertia
  • can change directions in mid-air
  • 10 HP
  • Instant death for falling to the bottom
  • Instant death for 0 HP
  • Touching the top deals 4 damage and pushes player down
  • Player is invincible for 2 seconds after taking damage

Game Properties

  • Platform rise speeds up over time
  • Platform speed starts at 5 seconds from bottom to top
  • Roughly 7-8 platforms are visible on-screen at the start of the game
  • Platform difficulty increases over time
  • Has a concept of "floors" which is your score, and roughly equates to the number of platforms that have gone off-screen divided by 5.

Platform Properties

  • Universal - width 28% of screen, can be stood on, landing on this heals 1 point of damage
  • Basic - no special
  • Conveyor - directional; while touching this platform, the player shifts either right or left slowly, walk speed while touching this platform is halved going against, or doubled going with the platform
  • Bouncy - landing on this propels the player upward a height equal to about a platform width, bounce is delayed by about 0.5 seconds
  • Spikes - landing on this deals the player 4 points of damage, only dealing additional damage if the player falls onto it again, otherwise this is a normal platform
  • Flip - this platform sops existing 0.5s after the player touches it

Emotion

  • Tense, Score driven, Suspenseful, Leap of faith

Lore Author Notes V4

23rd August 2020 at 10:48pm
V4

Intention:

The story should promote three possible (and irreconcilable) narratives (and a fourth bonus narrative):

Narrative 1: Apocalypse Narrative The pandemic is real, the Emergency Psychoepidemiology Research Center in Arkham University is real, but Dr. White is hallucinating some unrealistic things (due to stress, drugs, or WMP)

Narrative 2: Dream Narrative Dr. White is real, but Dr. White is dreaming / imagining all of the events of the game, including the pandemic. The story is a large metaphor for the emotional experience of guilt that Dr. White experiences having let down a patient who trusted them.

Narrative 3: Wonderland Narrative The "ordinary" world is an illusion, a small part of a larger metaphysical universe in which Wonderland is real.

Bonus Narrative: Meta Narrative This is all a videogame

Canonically, the correct answer should be left up to interpretation, however, if a contradiction is inevitable, the other interpretations should defer to Narrative 3 (Wonderland).

Note on Special Items

V1

One of the things that Lewis Carol does is subvert your expectation of what ordinary things do. Like cakes, but when you eat them you shrink. To play on this, we should include items that seem useless in a survival situation, but actually open up routes to alternate story lines, such as the “Eat Me” Cake, or a writing desk that you can trade for a Raven. I think it would be fun if you could not only start with these surprising items, but also find them as you scavenge.

Notes on pacing

26th March 2020 at 6:44am

How often does the player want to read? (never)

  • In the interest of limiting text, going forward I'm cutting all of the writing.
  • Narrative will be replaced with per-day Captioned Sketches and a map sketch
    • captions will be no more than one sentence
    • story will be less clear now
    • we still need to figure out what events transpire on each day, but the player does not have to read the full explanation.
    • need to communicate to the player "what is my character doing" without words

Repeating narrative pattern and Omen flow

  • The emotion of satisfaction packing for the right things usually comes when you have all your choices up front, then having your choices "pay off" later
  • To accomplish the above, the game needs to "frontload" the choices such that you are preparing for multiple Omens in a single "turn".
  • If we do one omen per turn, the game has a slow pacing...

Progression

This sort "descent into madness" story is more effective if rolled out slowly. As such; the first quarter of the story should be almost entirely free of supernatural elements, whereas the last quarter should have many.

Project Rabbit Hole

16th August 2020 at 11:15pm
V2 V4

"Rabbit Hole" was based on the principle of nested quarantines.

The infected were kept at the bottom, and information spread upward in steps. At each step, sanity was tested by comparing answers to basic questionnaires filled out by the researchers on the outside as well as the quarantined. If the majority concurred with the quarantined, they were permitted to pass to the next ring. If not, the quarantined were executed.

By this method, Wonderland was shielded from the outer researchers by use of the inner researchers, in the same way that haz-mat suits shield workers from radiation.

After much great sacrifice, we succeeded in compiling a confidential collection of all writings and images known to cause infection; the so-called "Wonderland Files".

No one outside could ever read the files, and therefore, no one on the "sane" research team knew what the files contained. For all we knew, they could have been blank.

Additional brave volunteers ventured in to conduct tests on the files, probing their virality through image compression, partial exposure, and similar tests. Results coming back were confusing, seemingly inconsistent.

Even now, it's hard to say whether we ever would have made a break through if The Breach hadn't happened. I'd like to think we would have.

From the journal of Dr. Raymond Burr

Stress

14th August 2020 at 10:47pm
V2

What is it?
The equivalent of "health" in other games.
The stress meter is above the story section, and is indicated by “hand-drawn” tally marks.
You have 5 "stress" that you can afford before you have to "restart" the game.

Sources of Stress:
Food
If you do not have one food at the end of the day, you gain one stress

Omens
If you do not meet the requirements from an omen, you take stress equal to the omen number. (first omen = 1, second omen = 2, etc.)

Food
If you do not have a food item at the end of the day, you add one stress.
If you do have a food item, it is consumed. The words "Ate {name of thing}." appear at the end of each diary entry.

Omens
See Omens
If you see three instances of an Omen
- you have to meet inventory requirements from the Omen.
- if you fail, you take stress equal to the omen number. (first omen = 1, second omen = 2, etc.)
- the counter for that type of omen resets to zero.

The Major Plot V2

17th August 2020 at 2:42am
V2

Who is Alice?

Alice is patient 0 of Wonderland. She was a psychiatric patient of the PC some eight years prior. She didn't want to give up her Wonderland then, and she doesn't now either.

Who is the Narrator / Player Character?

Dr. White. (name is a play on the "white rabbit") A psychiatrist and researcher. At one point attempted to help Alice with her psychotic breaks. Advised her to dismiss Wonderland as a delusion, (somewhat accidentally) planted the seed of the idea that caused Alice to make Wonderland contagious: that reality is what everyone agrees on.

Note: do not use pronouns anywhere in game text, do not specify gender.

What is really going on

The Wonderland pandemic is real, not a delusion. But there are some parts of Dr. White's observations that are delusional. Dr. White is mad / infected. As a result, they are forced to mentally relive the same days over and over, with only their diary as evidence that the events have transpired already.

A side effect of this is that s/he has the ability to speak directly to the Wonderland characters, even though they kinda don't exist. As for how the wonderland characters work, their parts are played by other people who have been infected with the same collective hallucination.

How does the game end

In order to gain a meeting with Alice, the PC has to gain entrance to her home. To do so, s/he must obtain certain items.

Note: it would be good here to have the items be kept in the players inventory because they have good stats, not because the player is forced to keep them. That way, the impact of the final reveal is greater, since the meta-narrative will align with the diagetic one (that Alice is pulling the strings)

These "important" items are all "imaginary" objects that are important to Alice's Wonderland Story, like the Vorpal Blade (a kitchen knife) and the Caterpillar's Hookah (a hookah).

The PC meets Alice, and she confronts him/her, breaking the fourth wall and speaking directly to the player.

Alice calls the player "the Red King", and goes on to say that the player is "dreaming". She says that when the dream ends, she will "go out—bang!—like a candle". "Just how tweedle-dee and tweedle-dum said."

She then goes on to say that before you "wake up" you should make a choice about how your dream will end. She explains that the gameplay of this game is all about deciding what is important to bring with you, and leaving the rest behind. She asks if the player thinks she should keep Wonderland, or throw it all away.

The player then gets an item that represents Wonderland, and can choose whether to keep it in his/her inventory or leave it behind. Depending on the player's choice, a different epilogue message plays, both epilogue messages are in the form of a "publisher" who has found and published Dr. White's journal, presumably posthumously.

The Mock Turtle

17th August 2020 at 3:53am

Mock Turtle Soup was a popular victorian soup dish said to imitate Green Turtle Soup in texture, without the need for actual turtle meat. The soup instead used calf brains or calf hooves or other organ meats. Visually, the mock turtle has a turtle shell but a cow's face and other cow parts.

The Mock Turtle is a very melancholic character, because it is thought that he used to be a real turtle. He tells Alice his history of going to school in the sea, but cannot understand the school system that Alice describes to him—least of all the poetry she recites. Alice cannot understand it either. This is a pun on the two meanings of "school", referring in the turtle's usage to a school of fish or marine animals, and by Alice to an institute of learning (see school). He says his teacher was an old Sea Turtle called Tortoise and when Alice asks him why he was called Tortoise if he was a Turtle the Mock Turtle answers, "We called him tortoise because he taught us!" ('tortoise' and 'taught us' both being pronounced <tɔːtʊs> in Carroll's dialect).

Another metaphor for deceptions or things which are not what they are.

Thematic Exploration

24th August 2020 at 1:27am
V5

Write What You Know

I think at the heart of this, I'm trying to share with the player my experience of knowing that you are crazy, but being unable to do anything about it.

Why Is That Fun?

Well, it's not.

So Why?

I want it to be a really valuable thought-exploration of a difficult (and (I believe) universal) subject in a safe space.

Specifically: I want to challenge the statements "I can trust ___"

  1. consensus-reality
  2. my senses
  3. my memories
  4. reason
  5. this game

See Breaking Expectations

The Question

The main question asked by this story is:

How do you decide what reality is?

The Plot

The plot explores the consequences of a pivotal conversation in which Dr. White tells Alice that "reality is what others say it is." (consensus reality)

The consequence being that Alice takes this to heart, and sets about converting everyone to agree on the existence of Wonderland (causing an apocalypse of madness).

Central Conflict

So we have our conflict: will Dr. White, or the player, accept that Wonderland is real now that everyone says it is, or will they cling to the idea of a rational reality when they are the only one who believes in it?

My hope is to address all of the major philosophical arguments for-and-against knowledge of a physical reality through the Wonderland characters.

Platonic Ideals

The world of Wonderland kind of works a bit like Plato's world of ideas, which is interesting because that can be a fun way to make it kind of come back full circle, with there being no clear right answer.

Aristotle says that Plato is wrong, Descartes says that Aristotle is wrong, Kant says that Descartes is wrong etc.

Rationalism

Descartes argues that perception cannot be relied upon, but reason and rationality can. "The angles of a triangle always add up to 180 degrees no matter what you perceive."

As a source material, Alice's Adventures in Wonderland provides the idea of madness. In Wonderland, the impossible - such as a triangle with more than 180 degrees - is commonplace.

In the first stage, the subject experiences no symptoms and is a carrier. This stage lasts about a week.

In the second stage: the subject begins conversing with hallucinations. The subject is often convinced of strange delusions, and desires to perform nonsensical actions.This phase lasts one to two weeks.

In the third stage, the subject has a complete break with reality as we know it. The subject can no longer recall anything about themselves, their family, or their past. They often declare a new name, like "The Five of Spades", or "Haigha". This phase has no known end point.

Subjects are not known to recover from their delusions, nor do they stop being contagious.
