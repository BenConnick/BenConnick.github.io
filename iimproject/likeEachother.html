"It took us so many tries just to get people to like each other. And this is all just for the emotion there."

At D.I.C.E. 2013, Jenova Chen explained the challenges of designing a game where the experience relied on players - total strangers - liking each other. It was a goal that some would say contrasts with human nature. Chen disagrees. He believes that players are not fundamentally bad, but it is the games that make them behave badly. He argues that players seek out meaningful interactions, and oftentimes, the most meaningful thing a player can do is to exercise their power over another player.

The experience of Journey relies on players never harming each other's immersion. Because of this, some very unusual design decisions were made. For one thing, player avatars do not display any kind of name, nor is there any sort of leaderboard or menu where the player can find the identity of the person they were playing with. In order to maintain this complete anonymity, players are prevented from inviting their friends or knowing which game they are joining.

No chat functions were included in the game, and the main method of communication provided was through “chirps” (sounds the player can make, which resemble the whistle of a flute), which were carefully crafted so that they could not be “spammed” or used offensively. Obviously, removing the typical channels of communication prevented players from using offensive language, but it also prevented them from breaking the immersion, as the entire game features no symbolic “language” in the usual sense. (the glyphs have little to no symbolic significance to players, and cannot be employed by players to communicate).

A seemingly minor decision was to completely remove player collisions. There is no way for the players to harmfully trap or push each other. Anyone who has played a game where player collisions are abused will realize that this is a huge part of maintaining the peace between players. Another decision that Chen speaks about was the choice to make collectibles appear to each player individually, so that one player cannot prevent another from collecting resources. Originally, the experience was intended to include four players, but Journey’s designers reduced this to two in order to create a greater connection between the two travelers, otherwise alone in the desert.

Making the players important to each other was another hurdle that the designers overcame by making players and other “animals” in the game increase each other’s “charge” or “power” when in proximity. In addition, the player’s “chirps” also “charge” other players. This means that the actions typical of other games, like swinging a sword or shooting a gun, are replaced with helping actions, but they are still equally interesting to the actor and the target.

To highlight this mechanics principle, the designers made the character without arms. A character without arms is not expected to be able to hit, restrain, or molest another.

By upholding design with theme, the experience is cohesive and unforgettable. Journey achieves the emotion Chen intended.
